Mahatma Gandhi

About Us


  • Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi (ISO: Mōhanadāsa Karamacaṁda Gāṁdhī: 2 October 1869 - 30 January 1948) was an Indian lawyer, anti-colonial nationalist and political ethicist who employed nonviolent resistance to lead the successful campaign for India's independence from British rule.

  • He inspired movements for civil rights and freedom across the world. The honorific Mahātmā (from Sanskrit 'great-souled, venerable'), first applied to him in South Africa in 1914, is now used throughout the world.

  • Born and raised in a Hindu family in coastal Gujarat, Gandhi trained in the law at the Inner Temple in London and was called to the bar in June 1891, at the age of 22.

  • After two uncertain years in India, where he was unable to start a successful law practice, Gandhi moved to South Africa in 1893 to represent an Indian merchant in a lawsuit. He went on to live in South Africa for 21 years.

  • There, Gandhi raised a family and first employed nonviolent resistance in a campaign for civil rights. In 1915, aged 45, he returned to Indiaand soon set about organising peasants, farmers, and urban labourers to protest against discrimination and excessive land-tax.

  • Returned to India in 1915 and joined the Indian National Congress. Advocated for India's independence from British rule.

  • Led various movements like Non-Cooperation, Civil Disobedience, and Quit India. Emphasized nonviolent resistance and civil disobedience as strategies for change. Stressed self-reliance, simplicity, and communal harmony in his philosophy.

  • Inspired millions globally with his principles of nonviolence. Assassinated on January 30, 1948, by Nathuram Godse. Remembered as a champion of truth, nonviolence, and social equality.

  • He undertook several fasts unto death to protest against violence and promote peace. Gandhi's Salt March in 1930 was a significant act of civil disobedience against British salt taxes.

  • He was imprisoned multiple times for his activism but remained steadfast in his principles. Gandhi's approach to conflict resolution and his emphasis on moral values continue to inspire movements for social change worldwide.

  • He was posthumously honored with the title "Mahatma," meaning "great soul," by the Indian people.

  • Gandhi's birthday, October 2nd, is celebrated as Gandhi Jayanti, a national holiday in India, and is observed globally as the International Day of Non-Violence.

  • Gandhi is considered to be the Father of the Nation in post-colonial India. During India's nationalist movement and in several decades immediately after, he was also commonly called Bapu
